The Brent/WTI spread is the market's real-time verdict on US energy independence. The +15% Brent premium means the rest of the world (including Australia) is paying materially more for oil than the US. Combined with Oman FOB at $167 vs WTI at $97, Australia is experiencing a **three-tier price structure**:
- WTI $97 (US domestic, irrelevant to Australia)
- Brent ~$112 (global benchmark, some relevance)
- Oman FOB $167 (what actually flows to Singapore refiners → Australia)

### Key Events Documented
1. **Israel → South Pars** (Iran's largest gas field, shared with Qatar's North Field — largest gas field in the world)
2. **Iran → Ras Laffan** (Qatar's LNG export hub — processes ~77M tonnes/year, ~20% of global LNG supply)
3. This escalates the conflict from an oil disruption to a **gas disruption** — two separate commodity shocks simultaneously

### Strategic Reserve Context
- US SPR release: **172 million barrels** (crude oil, confirmed)
- IEA coordinated release: ~400M barrels total (from Edition 7 daily brief)
- These are oil releases — do not address the LNG/gas market shock

### Analytical Significance for Assessment

**The "Two Crises" structure:**
The conflict has produced not one but two simultaneous energy crises:
1. **Oil crisis** — Hormuz closure, tanker attacks, crude supply offline
2. **Gas crisis** — South Pars + Ras Laffan strikes; LNG supply disrupted; European gas +160%

**Australia's dual position (LNG exporter / fuel importer):**
- Australia is one of the world's largest LNG exporters (Woodside, Santos, INPEX projects in WA/NT/QLD)
- LNG prices doubling → **Australian LNG exporters are recording windfall revenues**
- BUT Australia is almost entirely dependent on **imported liquid fuels** (petrol, diesel, jet fuel)
- Result: Australia simultaneously wins on gas exports and loses on fuel imports — asymmetric exposure
- The winners (Woodside shareholders) are not the same as the losers (regional truckers, farmers, airlines)

**Japan, South Korea, Taiwan — Australia's LNG customers:**
- These are Australia's primary LNG export markets
- They are also the countries most exposed to the oil price spike (no domestic production)
- Their demand for Australian LNG is surging (substitute for disrupted Middle East gas)
- Short-term: Australian LNG is more valuable than ever
- Medium-term: Demand destruction in these economies (recession) will eventually reduce LNG demand

**The Qatar-Australia parallel:**
- Both are major LNG exporters; both are US treaty partners
- Qatar's Ras Laffan being struck while Australia's Northwest Shelf projects continue unharmed = Australian LNG premium in the market
- But also raises the question: are Australian LNG facilities now a potential target in any wider conflict?

### Analytical Significance — The Inflation Transmission Gap

This model is US-centric (WTI-based). It **understates Australia's inflation exposure** by design.

**The benchmark problem for Australia's inflation:**
- US inflation driven by WTI ($97 and rising)
- Australian inflation driven by Singapore MOPS / Oman FOB ($138-167)
- Ratio: $167 / $97 = **1.72x** — Australia's crude input cost is 72% higher than the US model assumes

**Scaling the model for Australia:**
If WTI $150 → US CPI 4.3%, then Australia (paying Oman equivalent) already faces the energy cost shock equivalent to a WTI ~$167 world. The Ironclad daily brief (Ed5, 17 Mar) already assessed Australian inflation heading to **mid-high 4%** — consistent with this model, but arrived there faster than the US will.

**Australia reached US's "worst case scenario" (WTI $150 = CPI 4.3%) before the US did**, because Australia pays the Oman price, not the WTI price.

**The inflation differential has policy implications:**
- US Fed may pause rate hikes if CPI stays at 3.2-3.5% (within tolerated range)
- RBA faces structurally higher inflation than the Fed from the same conflict
- This creates **AUD/USD divergence pressure** — RBA forced to hike while Fed holds
- Capital flows away from AUD if rate differential compresses; import costs rise further; inflation compounds

**Historical parallel — 1973:**
In the 1973 embargo, all major economies experienced roughly the same inflationary shock because all were exposed to the same global oil price. In 2026, the inflationary shock is differentiated by benchmark exposure. The US is shielded at 3.2-3.5%; Australia is already at 4%+. This asymmetry has no direct historical precedent.
