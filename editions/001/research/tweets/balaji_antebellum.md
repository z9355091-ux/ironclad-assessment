> "I'm going to make some obvious points.
>
> (1) Blowing up all the oil infrastructure in the Middle East is an insane idea, and may well result in a global economic crash and humanitarian crisis unrivaled in the lives of those now living. We're talking about the price of everything everywhere rising, from food to gas, at a moment when inflation was already high. All of that will be laid at the feet of the authors of this war.
>
> (2) The antebellum status quo of Feb 27, 2026 was just not that bad, but we're unlikely to return to it. **Expect indefinite, long-term, ongoing disruptions to everything out of the Middle East.**
>
> (3) Also assume tech financing crashes for the indefinite future. The genius plan to get the Gulf states caught in the crossfire has incinerated much of the funding for LPs, for datacenters, and for IPOs. Anyone in tech who supported this war may soon learn the meaning of 'force majeure' as funding gets yanked.
>
> (4) Many capital allocators will instead be allocating much further down Maslow's hierarchy of needs, towards useful basic things like food and energy.
>
> (5) It's fortunate that all those progressives yelled about the 'climate crisis.' Yes, their reasoning about timelines was wrong, and much of the money was wasted in graft, but **the result was right: we all need energy independence from the Middle East, pronto.** It's also fortunate that Elon and China autistically took climate seriously. Now they're going to need to ship a billion solar panels, electric vehicles, batteries, nuclear power plants, and the like to get everyone off oil, immediately.
>
> (6) It's not just an oil and gas problem, of course. It's also a **fertilizer problem, and a chemical precursor problem.** Maybe some new sources will come online at the new prices, but it takes time to dial stuff up, particularly at this scale, so **shortages are almost a certainty.** That said, China has actually scaled up coal-to-chemicals (C2C), and there's also something more sci-fi called Power-to-X which turns arbitrary power + water + air into hydrocarbons. But all of that will need to get accelerated.
>
> (7) Ultimately, this war is going to result in tremendous blame for anyone associated with it. It's a no-win scenario to blow up this much infrastructure for so many people. Simply not worth it for whatever objective they thought they were going to attain. But unless you're actually in a position to stop the madness, **the pragmatic thing to do is: scramble to mitigate the fallout to yourself, your business, and your people.**

## Analytical Significance for Assessment

### The "antebellum" frame is the right one for the Assessment
Balaji's "antebellum" framing is analytically precise and should be adopted in the Assessment. Previous oil shocks (1973, 1979, 1990) were disruptions to an otherwise stable system — the system recovered and resumed. The argument here (from both Bertrand and Balaji independently) is that 2026 is a structural break, not a disruption. The pre-war energy order is not returning.

For Australia this means:
- 30-day reserve policy calibrated to a disruption-tolerant world; now needs to be recalibrated for indefinite instability
- Refinery closure decisions made under "stable global supply" assumptions need to be revisited
- Energy security policy built around "just-in-time" import logistics is structurally inadequate for the post-antebellum world

### Gulf state LP withdrawal — Australian implications
- Australian superannuation funds and government investment vehicles have indirect exposure to tech funds with Gulf LP bases
- More directly: Australian tech sector (StartupAus, etc.) will face tighter global capital conditions
- The Maslow rotation (from growth to basics) benefits Australian commodity exporters; hurts Australian tech/startup ecosystem

### The climate transition argument (for the Assessment's policy section)
If the energy transition was always going to be justified on security grounds eventually, the 2026 crisis has provided the forcing function — years ahead of any policy timeline. Australian energy policy recommendations (reserve expansion, refinery revival, import diversification) are no longer abstract resilience measures; they are the obvious response to a structural shift that is "not going away."
