# 1979 Iranian Revolution Oil Shock

## Crisis Overview

**Trigger/Cause:** Iranian Revolution (Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi overthrown Jan 16, 1979 → Ayatollah Khomeini takes power) + Iran-Iraq War begins 1980

**Duration:** 1979-1980 (primary shock); effects extended into mid-1980s

**Key Players:**
- Iran: 6 million barrels/day reduced to 1.5 million barrels/day (Nov 1978 strike at oil refineries)
- Saudi Arabia & OPEC: Attempted offset with increased production, but insufficient
- Russia rejected OPEC+ cuts (precursor to 2020 dynamics)
- Iraq: War disruption in 1980 caused additional 7% worldwide production decline

## Price Impact

- **Pre-crisis:** $15.85/barrel (April 1979)
- **Peak:** $39.50/barrel (Dec 1979) within 12 months
- **Percentage increase:** ~150% increase
- **Global production loss:** ~4% overall (global supply relatively stable due to production offsets)
- **Real price record:** Highest real price until March 3, 2008 (oil reached $248/m³)

## Global Economic Impact

- **Recession:** Economic recessions worldwide, particularly severe in industrialized nations
- **Stagflation:** Inflation + stagnation combination
- **Demand response:** Panic buying at gas stations (echoing 1973 memories)
- **Vehicle idling waste:** Americans estimated wasting 150,000 barrels/day idling in gas station lines
- **Industrial restructuring:** Electric utilities switched from oil to coal, natural gas, nuclear power
- **Technology shift:** Commercial exploration developed major non-OPEC oilfields (Siberia, Alaska, North Sea, Gulf of Mexico)
- **Market power shift:** By 1986, daily worldwide demand fell 5M bpd; non-OPEC production rose more, shrinking OPEC market share from 50% (1979) to 29% (1985)

## US Domestic Impact

- **Carter administration:** Phased deregulation of oil prices (April 5, 1979)
- **Political response:** "Crisis of Confidence" speech (July 15, 1979), "malaise" speech; installed solar panels on White House roof
- **Policy:** Carter Doctrine declared Persian Gulf control attempt "assault on vital interests of US" (Jan 1980)
- **Industry:** Domestic oil prices remained at $6/barrel while world market was $30/barrel
- **Windfall profit tax:** Proposed on oil companies
- **Synthetic Fuels Corporation:** Established (1980) to develop alternatives
- **Price controls:** Repealed gradually, allowing US oil output to rise sharply from Prudhoe Bay fields; oil imports fell sharply
- **Gasoline rationing:** Coupons printed but never used; several states implemented odd-even rationing (California, Pennsylvania, New York, New Jersey, Oregon, Texas)

## Automobile Industry Transformation

- **Japanese surge:** Japanese manufacturers surpassed Detroit's production totals within a year of 1979
- **Market share:** Japanese cars rose from 9% (1976) to 21% (1980) of US auto purchases
- **CAFE standards:** Corporate Average Fuel Economy mandates drove downsizing
- **Model shifts:** Citation, Fairmont, Aries replaced larger models; Toyota Corolla, VW Rabbit dominated imports
- **Technology:** Fuel injection, multi-valve engines replaced carburetors
- **Fuel economy:** US average improved from ~15 mpg (1979) to 18 mpg (1985) to 20 mpg (1990)

## Policy Responses (Global)

- **Industrial nations:** Multibillion-dollar research programs for oil alternatives
- **Energy diversification:** Non-OPEC oilfield exploration accelerated (North Sea, Alaska, Siberia, Gulf of Mexico)
- **OPEC adaptation:** Internal divisions emerged; Saudi Arabia as "swing producer" attempted market share recovery post-1985

## Key Insights

- Global supply disruption was only ~4%, but market psychology (panic) and Iran-Iraq War prospects created price multiplication
- Triggered fundamental Western energy independence strategy
- Accelerated Japanese automotive dominance over US Big Three
- Created long-term structural shift away from OPEC dependence via diversification
