# 1990 Gulf War (Kuwait Invasion)

## Crisis Overview

**Trigger/Cause:** Iraqi invasion of Kuwait on August 2, 1990 by Saddam Hussein (second OPEC invasion)

**Duration:** August 1990 – March 1991 (9 months, shorter than 1973 or 1979)

**Root Causes:**
- Iraq owed Kuwait $14 billion in war loans from Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988)
- Iraq believed Kuwait was overproducing oil, lowering prices and hurting Iraqi profits
- Iraq claimed Kuwait was stealing its oil via slant drilling

## Price Impact

- **July 1990:** $17/barrel (pre-invasion baseline)
- **August 6, 1990:** $28/barrel (immediate impact)
- **October 1990 peak:** $46/barrel (mid-October peak, highest point)
- **Percentage increase:** ~170% from baseline
- **Duration impact:** Most short-lived of major oil crises (9 months vs 6+ months for 1973, 1979)

## Global Economic Impact

- **Recession:** Contributed to early 1990s US recession (less severe than 1973 or 1979 due to shorter duration)
- **Confidence effect:** Rapid military success (Desert Storm) restored market confidence quickly
- **Kuwaiti oil fires:** Set by retreating Iraqi forces in 1991; not fully extinguished until November 1991; took years for combined Iraqi-Kuwaiti production to regain pre-invasion levels

## Supply Disruption

- **Combined Iraq-Kuwait production:** 4.3 million barrels/day at risk
- **Threat:** Potential Saudi Arabian production loss (major concern mitigated by US military success)
- **Duration:** Only 9 months until market confidence restored; prices began falling as US military advantage became clear

## US Financial Response

- **Federal Reserve decision:** Did NOT raise interest rates despite inflation risk (contrasted with 1973/1979 response)
- **Rationale:** Confidence in Desert Storm military success + desire to maintain long-term policy credibility built in 1980s
- **Fiscal response:** Gramm-Rudman-Hollings Balanced Budget Act revised to allow deficit adjustments for economic shocks
- **Result:** Peak at $46/bbl mid-October, followed by steady decline through 1994

## Key Insights

- **Shorter shock:** 9 months vs 6+ months in prior crises = faster market recovery
- **Military deterrence:** Rapid US-led coalition success reassured markets about long-term supply security
- **Fed credibility:** Decision to hold rates steady (not tighten) proved effective + reflected confidence
- **Precedent:** Showed that military action can shorten energy crisis duration by restoring confidence in supply security
- **Geographic vulnerability:** Kuwait + Iraq combined only ~4% of world oil, but location (Near East) + Saudi proximity created disproportionate impact
