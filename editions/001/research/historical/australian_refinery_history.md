# Australia's Refinery History: From ~8 Refineries to 2

## Overview

Australia has experienced a dramatic structural decline in domestic refining capacity over the past 15 years (2010-2026), collapsing from approximately 8 refineries to just 2 currently operating. This represents a ~75% capacity reduction and creates critical vulnerability to supply chain disruptions.

## Refinery Closures Timeline

### Shell Clyde Refinery (Sydney)
- **Closure date:** 2012
- **Status:** Converted (from refinery to import-focused terminal)
- **Capacity loss:** ~60,000 bpd
- **Reason:** Economics of domestic refining; shift to import-based fuel supply model

### Caltex Kurnell Refinery (Sydney, NSW)
- **Closure date:** 2014
- **Owner:** Caltex Petroleum Limited
- **Status:** Closed
- **Capacity loss:** ~120,000 bpd
- **Reason:** Uneconomic vs. importing refined products; cheaper to import than refine domestically
- **Note:** Kurnell was a major refinery serving Sydney metro area

### BP Bulwer Island Refinery (Brisbane, Queensland)
- **Closure date:** 2015
- **Owner:** British Petroleum
- **Status:** Closed
- **Capacity loss:** ~90,000 bpd
- **Reason:** Low oil prices (2014-2015 glut period); uncompetitive refining margins
- **Location:** Bulwer Island, Brisbane

### Shell Geelong Refinery (Victoria)
- **Closure date:** 2021
- **New owner:** Viva Energy (acquired 2017, operated briefly before closure)
- **Status:** Converted to import-focused terminal (Viva Energy Geelong)
- **Capacity loss:** ~150,000 bpd
- **Reason:** Uneconomic refining operation; Viva Energy pivot to distribution model
- **Note:** Was one of Australia's largest refineries

### Ampol Lytton Refinery (Brisbane, Queensland)
- **Status:** STILL OPERATING (2026)
- **Capacity:** ~130,000 bpd (estimated, current)
- **Owner:** Ampol Limited
- **Location:** Brisbane area
- **Note:** One of two remaining operational refineries in Australia

## Currently Operating Refineries (2026)

### 1. Ampol Lytton (Brisbane, Queensland)
- **Capacity:** ~130,000 bpd
- **Status:** Operating
- **Strategic role:** Critical for East Coast fuel supply

### 2. Viva Energy Geelong (Victoria)
- **Capacity:** ~140,000 bpd
- **Status:** Operating
- **Strategic role:** Critical for South East Australia (Victorian/Tasmanian supply)
- **Owner:** Viva Energy (ASX-listed)

**Total Current Domestic Refining Capacity:** ~270,000 bpd (down from ~700,000+ bpd in 2010)

## Government Response: Fuel Security Package 2021

### Key Measures:
1. **Strategic fuel reserves:** Establishment of emergency oil stocks
2. **Fuel import diversity:** Encouraging imports from multiple sources (Singapore, South Korea, China refineries)
3. **Support for remaining refineries:** Regulatory measures to support economic viability of Ampol & Viva Energy
4. **Supply chain resilience:** Stockpiling requirements and contractual arrangements for offshore reserves

### IEA Obligations vs. Australian Reality:
- **IEA Standard:** 90-day stockholding obligation
- **Australia's compliance:** ~30 days onshore + contractual offshore arrangements (non-compliant with 90-day standard for decades)
- **Exposure:** Significant vulnerability to supply chain disruptions

## Drivers of Refinery Closures

### Economic Factors:
1. **Refining margins:** Australian refineries have higher operating costs than Asian competitors (Singapore, South Korea, China)
2. **Fuel import competitiveness:** Cheaper to import refined products from Southeast Asia than operate local refineries
3. **Capital intensity:** Old refineries require significant capex for maintenance; new builds prohibitively expensive
4. **Scale disadvantage:** Australian market (25M people) too small to support multiple large efficient refineries vs. Asian continent-scale operations
5. **Crude oil import costs:** All crude must be imported; no cost advantage vs. importing finished products

### Market Conditions:
1. **2010-2015 oil glut:** Global oversupply reduced refining margins
2. **2020 COVID crash:** Accelerated uneconomic refinery closures
3. **Asian competition:** Singapore alone has 1.4M bpd refining capacity; Vietnam, South Korea, China all have large efficient refineries

### Policy/Regulatory:
1. **Labor cost environment:** Australian labor costs higher than Asia
2. **Environmental compliance:** Increasingly stringent local emission/environmental standards add costs
3. **Import liberalization:** Free trade agreements facilitated shift to import model

## Supply Chain Vulnerabilities

### Current State:
- **Domestic production:** ~70% of fuel is imported (refined products, some crude)
- **Import sources:** Singapore (~40%), South Korea (~20%), China refineries (~15%), other sources (~25%)
- **Tanker fleet:** Dependent on global maritime logistics
- **Logistics chokepoints:**
  - Strait of Malacca (between Malaysia/Indonesia) - critical chokepoint for Asia-Australia oil flows
  - Suez Canal (alternative for Middle East oil, longer route)

### Crisis Vulnerabilities:
1. **Supply chain length:** Australia at end of ~4,000+ nautical mile supply chain from Singapore/Middle East
2. **Single-source risk:** If Singapore refineries disrupted (8M+ bpd at risk from single point of failure)
3. **Storage limits:** Strategic reserves at 30 days onshore (IEA standard 90 days) means limited buffer
4. **Refinery overcapacity Asia:** Asian refinery overcapacity has made Australian domestic refining uncompetitive
5. **Iran war / Strait of Hormuz risk:** 
   - Normal Strait of Hormuz flow ~20M bpd
   - If disrupted, would spike prices for all oil imports
   - Australia faces dual hit: (1) price spike, (2) potential supply loss if alternative routes congested

## Historical Context: Refinery Status at Time of Past Crises

### 1973 Arab Oil Embargo:
- Australia had 6-8 operational refineries at the time
- Domestic refining capacity sufficient for majority of fuel needs
- More resilient to supply chain disruptions (lower import dependency)

### 1979 Iranian Revolution:
- Australia still had 5-6 operational refineries
- Growing import dependency but still 40-50% domestically refined
- More resilient position than 2026

### 1990 Gulf War:
- Australia had 4-5 refineries; import dependency rising
- Market conditions favored domestic refining economics
- Supply chain more manageable

### 2020 COVID Crash:
- Only Ampol Lytton & Viva Energy Geelong operating
- 2 remaining refineries under severe economic pressure
- High import dependency (70%+)

## Strategic Implications for 2026 Iran War / Strait of Hormuz Disruption

**Critical vulnerability:** Australia now depends on **2 aging refineries** in an era of potential Strait of Hormuz disruption. If:

1. **Oil prices spike** (to $100-150+/bbl): Direct impact on all imports
2. **Supply chain diverted:** Alternative routes (Suez) or sources add 2-3 weeks to deliveries
3. **Asian refinery disruption:** Singapore, South Korea refineries hit by Iranian missiles/disruption → refined product shortage
4. **Strategic reserves exhaustion:** 30-day onshore supply + contractual offshore could be exhausted in 4-6 weeks of disruption

**Current setup resembles pre-1973 vulnerability** but with zero domestic refining buffer once Ampol & Viva Energy reach capacity or face disruption themselves.
