# Current Situation: Australia's Energy Vulnerability (March 2026)

## Strait of Hormuz Flow & Disruption Status

### Normal Operations:
- **Typical flow:** ~20 million barrels per day (confirmed 2023-2025)
- **LNG component:** ~20% of world's LNG passes through annually
- **Oil component:** ~25% of seaborne oil trade

### Current 2026 Iran War Status (March 2026):
- **Closure status:** De facto closure as of March 2, 2026 (IRGC official announcement)
- **Shipping activity:** Dropped sharply Feb 28 - March 12
- **Mines deployed:** ~12 mines confirmed March 12, 2026
- **Insurance spikes:** 4-6x normal rates (quarter-million $ per tanker transit)
- **International response:** Trump announced U.S. Navy escort of tankers "as soon as possible"
- **Diplomatic efforts:** European & Asian allies rejected military coalition; seeking diplomatic solution

### Timeline Implications for Australia:
- **Normal sea distance:** Singapore/Middle East to Australia: ~4,000-5,000 nautical miles
- **Disruption delay:** Additional 2-3 weeks if Suez/alternative routes required
- **Current stock buffer:** ~30 days onshore + contractual offshore
- **Risk window:** 30-day buffer depleted in 4-5 weeks of sustained disruption + transport delay

## Alternative Pipeline Capacity

### East-West Petroline (Saudi Arabia)
- **Capacity:** ~4 million barrels/day (per daily brief transcripts)
- **Route:** Traverses Saudi Arabia coast-to-coast (Persian Gulf to Red Sea)
- **Status:** Operational, but limited to Saudi volumes + Iraqi/Kuwaiti use
- **Limitations:** 
  - Only ~4M bpd vs 20M normal Hormuz flow
  - Depends on Saudi political cooperation
  - Does not substitute for full strait capacity

### Habshan–Fujairah Pipeline (UAE)
- **Capacity:** ~2 million barrels/day
- **Route:** Abu Dhabi fields to Fujairah terminal (bypasses Strait)
- **Storage:** World's largest crude oil storage facility (14M barrels) being built in Fujairah
- **Impact:** Reduces Strait dependency for UAE ~3/4 of its production, but insufficient for global needs

### Iraq Pipeline via Saudi Arabia (IPSA)
- **Capacity:** ~1.65 million barrels/day
- **Route:** Iraq across Saudi Arabia to Red Sea port
- **Status:** Reopened June 2012
- **Limitations:** Insufficient for global demand substitution

### Total Alternative Pipeline Capacity: ~7-8M bpd
- **vs. Normal Hormuz:** 20M bpd
- **Gap:** 12-13M bpd supply deficit if Strait closed
- **Australian impact:** Alternative routes add 2-3 weeks to delivery; limited volume available

## Australian Fuel Reserve Levels

### IEA 90-Day Obligation
- **Standard:** International Energy Agency requires 90 days of stockholding
- **Australia's actual:** ~30 days onshore + contractual offshore arrangements
- **Compliance status:** Non-compliant for **years** (per daily brief notes)
- **Exposure:** Significantly below safety threshold

### Breakdown of Current Reserves:
**Onshore (Physical Storage):**
- ~30 days of domestic consumption
- Locations: Multiple storage facilities across east coast
- Ampol & Viva Energy Geelong maintain working stocks
- Strategic reserve releases announced March 12 by IEA (400M barrels, though not all Australian allocation)

**Offshore (Contractual):**
- Arrangements with international suppliers (Singapore, South Korea, China refineries)
- Not fully specified in available materials
- Dependent on global supply availability
- Vulnerable to disruption at source or en route

### Consumption Rate & Runway:
- **Daily consumption:** Australia ~1M barrels/day (estimated, need verification)
- **At 30-day onshore level:** 30M barrels = 30 days at consumption
- **Crisis scenario:** If imports disrupted for 4+ weeks, shortage emerges
- **Mitigation window:** Strategic release would extend by 5-7 days, not solve underlying shortage

## Australian Fuel Import Sources

### Primary Import Origins:
- **Singapore:** ~40% of Australian refined products imports (estimated)
  - Singapore has 1.4M bpd refining capacity (among Asia's largest)
  - 3-4 day transit via Strait of Malacca to Australia
  
- **South Korea:** ~20% (estimated)
  - Modern large-scale refineries
  - 5-6 day transit via Strait of Malacca, Strait of Hormuz alternative
  
- **China refineries:** ~15% (estimated)
  - Multiple state-owned refineries
  - 6-7 day transit via various routes
  
- **Other sources:** ~25% (Middle East, other Asian, Africa)
  - Diverse mix; some via Hormuz, some via alternatives

### Supply Chain Vulnerabilities:
1. **Geographic concentration:** Singapore accounts for ~40%; single disruption point risk
2. **Transit route dependency:** Most routes pass through Strait of Malacca or Strait of Hormuz
3. **Refinery supply shock:** If Middle East production disrupted (South Pars Iran, Ras Laffan Qatar hit in current war), global refinery feedstock constraints
4. **Asian refinery disruption:** If Asian refineries suffer from mine risk/insurance costs, refinery economics worsen, export prices rise

### March 2026 War Impact on Supplies:
- **South Pars (Iran) struck:** Major LNG/condensate facility damaged/offline
- **Ras Laffan (Qatar) hit:** Major LNG facility impacted
- **LNG prices doubled** (per daily brief)
- **Asian reversion to coal:** Energy supply shock in Asia driving coal demand surge
- **Australia's coal exports surge:** Short-term benefit, but energy market volatility increases fuel costs

## Australian Pump Prices

### Pre-War (February 2026):
- **Average pump price:** ~$1.50-$1.60/liter (per daily brief transcripts)
- **Baseline:** Relatively stable, reflecting ~$70 Brent

### Current Situation (March 2026):
- **Average pump price:** ~$2.30/liter (per daily brief)
- **Increase:** ~$0.70-$0.80/liter = 45-50% spike in 2-3 weeks
- **Drivers:**
  1. Oil price spike ($70 → $100+/bbl = $0.50-$0.60/L)
  2. Supply chain disruption premiums (fuel to Australia costs more via alt routes)
  3. Insurance cost pass-through (shipping insurance 4-6x higher)
  4. Refinery margin expansion (marginal supply drives refinery margins up)
  5. Demand destruction psychology (hoarding behavior in some markets)

### Future Projections:
- **If oil reaches $150/bbl:** Pump prices could reach $2.80-$3.00/liter
- **Cost impact on consumers:** $0.50 fill-up → $0.70-$0.75 (40%+ increase)
- **Business impact:** Transport, logistics, manufacturing costs rise immediately

## Government Responses Taken to Date (March 2026)

### 1. Strategic Reserve Release
- **IEA coordinated release:** 400M barrels total (March 12, 2026)
- **Australia's allocation:** Not specified, likely <10% of global total (~20-40M barrels)
- **Duration impact:** ~1-2 months at Australian consumption rate
- **Announcement:** Demonstrates government action, but insufficient for extended disruption

### 2. Emission Standards Relaxation
- **Action:** Temporary relaxation of fuel quality/emission standards
- **Purpose:** Allow import of broader range of refined products (lower quality acceptable)
- **Impact:** Enables more suppliers to enter market; reduces supply constraints
- **Risk:** Environmental/air quality trade-off

### 3. ACCC Emergency Meeting
- **Action:** Australian Competition & Consumer Commission held emergency session
- **Focus:** Price gouging prevention, supply monitoring
- **Powers:** Limited; cannot set prices, can only investigate unconscionable conduct
- **Effectiveness:** Low — market forces dominate; price controls typically ineffective

### 4. Treasurer's Price Gouging Warning
- **Action:** Government warnings to retailers/wholesalers against "excessive" price increases
- **Legal basis:** Consumer law provisions on unconscionable conduct
- **Market impact:** Minimal; fuel is commodity; prices set by global markets, not retailer choice
- **Political: Message to voters; reassurance of government action

### 5. Fuel Security Package 2021 (Pre-existing)
- **Strategic reserves:** Onshore stocks expanded to ~30 days
- **Contractual reserves:** Offshore stockpiling arrangements established
- **Import diversity:** Policy emphasis on multiple supplier sources
- **Fuel Security Fund:** Government support for refinery viability (Ampol, Viva Energy)
- **IEA coordination:** Participation in IEA emergency reserves protocols

## Demand Destruction & Economic Impact

### Direct Impacts:
1. **Transportation costs:** Airlines, logistics, public transport affected immediately
2. **Manufacturing:** Energy-intensive industries (steel, chemicals, cement) face cost squeeze
3. **Household budgets:** Fuel is ~5-7% of household budget for low-income; 2-3% for high-income
4. **Wage pressure:** Transport workers may demand wage increases to offset costs

### Macro Impacts:
- **Inflation:** Fuel shock cascades to food (transport), goods (energy costs)
- **Interest rate pressure:** RBA may need to hold/raise rates despite growth concerns (stagflation trap)
- **Demand destruction:** High fuel costs reduce discretionary spending, tourism, leisure
- **GDP impact:** Daily brief notes "Australian inflation to mid-high 4%; rate hike priced in as US GDP halves"

### Regional Comparison:
- **China:** Weathers energy shock better than regional competitors (per daily brief) — state-owned capacity, long-term contracts
- **Japan, South Korea, India:** More vulnerable (100% import dependent for oil)
- **Australia:** Mid-range vulnerability — not as dependent as island nations, but 70% import dependent

## Critical Exposure: Taiwan Semiconductor Energy Dependency

### Taiwan's Energy Position:
- **Dependency:** 95% of energy imported (per daily brief)
- **Supply route:** Through Strait of Hormuz (normal) or alternatives
- **Semiconductor production:** Taiwan produces ~65% of world semiconductors, ~90% of advanced chips (<7nm)
- **Energy requirement:** Semiconductors are energy-intensive manufacturing

### Australia's Exposure:
- **Taiwan supply chain risk:** If Taiwan's semiconductor production disrupted, Australian tech sector affected
- **Indirect effect:** Taiwan energy shortage → production cuts → global chip shortage → Australian manufacturing, automotive, consumer electronics disrupted
- **Not priced in:** Per daily brief: "Taiwan's semiconductor energy dependency that no one in Canberra is yet pricing in"

## China's Strategic Position & Power Balance Shift

### China's Energy Position:
- **Weathers shock better than all regional competitors** (per daily brief)
- **Reasons:** 
  - State-owned energy companies; long-term contracts with Middle East, Russia
  - Domestic coal capacity (undersupply of coal globally → China benefits from diversified sources)
  - Oil reserves policy: government maintains strategic stock  
- **Market dynamics:** While allied economies weaken, China's relative position strengthens

### Power Balance Implications:
- **Strategic shift:** "The Iran war is quietly shifting the Indo-Pacific power balance toward China"
- **Allied framework assumption:** Australian foreign policy assumes rough parity among regional powers
- **If assumption fails:** "Every aspect of Australian foreign policy must be recalibrated"
- **Timing:** Trump-Xi summit delayed to 5-6 weeks (per daily brief) — US-China framework frozen through May 2026

## Gasoline vs. Diesel Availability

### Imbalance Risk:
- **Pre-war:** Australia imports ~70% refined products
- **During disruption:** Refinery outages + supply constraints = uneven product mix
- **Potential shortage:** Diesel shortage likely before gasoline shortage (more refineries produce gasoline; diesel supply more constrained)
- **Impact:** Heavy vehicle transport (trucks, logistics) disrupted if diesel unavailable
- **Critical sectors:** Agriculture (seasonal harvest), construction, mining supply chains

## Timeline to Resolution & Normalisation

### Short-term (March 2026 - May 2026):
- **Week 1-2:** Strait de facto closed; ship insurance 4-6x; limited shipping through
- **Week 3-4:** First minesweeping operations (US Navy + allies); initial mine clearing progress
- **Week 5-8:** Partial mine clearance; limited shipping resumes; insurance still elevated
- **Supply lag:** 4,000+ nm distance + transport delays = 4-5 weeks for new supply to reach Australia

### Medium-term (June 2026 - August 2026):
- **Mines mostly cleared:** U.S. military capability sufficient (General Dempsey 2012 statement: "we can defeat that")
- **Shipping resumes gradually:** Insurance costs decline; merchant confidence rebuilds
- **Supply normalization:** Australian import flows recover to 80-90% of pre-war levels
- **Price moderation:** Oil prices decline toward $80-100/bbl (elevated vs pre-war, but stable)

### Long-term Implications (Sep 2026+):
- **Australian policy recalibration:** Taiwan semiconductor risk, China power shift must be addressed
- **Strategic reserves:** Push for 60-90 day onshore stocks (vs current 30-day)
- **Refinery reconsideration:** Debate over closing last 2 refineries vs. maintaining strategic capacity
- **Trade diversification:** Reduced reliance on Asia; seek alternative suppliers (Africa, Australia production if possible)
