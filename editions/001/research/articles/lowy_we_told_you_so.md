# Lowy Institute — "Australia's fuel crisis? We told you so"

**Source:** Lowy Institute, The Interpreter  
**URL:** https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/australia-s-fuel-crisis-we-told-you-so  
**Date:** 2026 (published during current crisis)  
**Reliability:** HIGH — peer-reviewed think tank; primary source citations throughout

---

## Core Argument

Australia's fuel insecurity was entirely predictable and comprehensively predicted. No recent government can say it was not warned. The "fair-weather" policy approach to fuel security contrasts starkly with Australia's successful approach to critical minerals security.

---

## Key Claims

### The warning record
- **2014-15 Senate inquiry** into Australia's transport energy resilience explicitly examined current vulnerabilities
- **Engineers Australia** told the inquiry: "The policy equivalent of 'the cheque's in the mail' is worrying. Liquid fuel in transit to Australia through some of the world's geopolitical hotspots is not fuel security, it is wishful thinking."
- Organisations that forecast the current problem: Australia Institute, ASPI, Engineers Australia, Maritime Union of Australia, Lowy Institute, NRMA
- **2013 parliamentary report** on oil refinery industry concluded: "changes in domestic refining capacity to date will not impact on Australia meeting its liquid fuel requirements. There are reliable, mature and highly diversified international fuel supply chains." — catastrophically wrong

### The policy failure diagnosis
- "Everything is fine, until it is not — the same policy approach with the same predictable outcomes"
- "When it comes to national security, 'market-based solutions' do not age well"
- Industry will not pay for large-scale onshore fuel storage (not commercially rational)
- Government must do the heavy lifting — but expenditure on "something consistently assured to be unnecessary would not be well received by an agitated electorate"
- This is the structural political economy trap: the cost of prevention is visible; the cost of the crisis is deferred

### The New Zealand parallel
- NZ Foreign Minister Winston Peters: both Australia and NZ "ought to have known that some of their oil refineries should have been kept open"
- NZ now has **zero onshore refining capacity**
- Australia has only two refineries remaining
- Peters: both countries were "far too cocky about the world circumstance [they're] in"

### The critical minerals contrast (what good policy looks like)
- EU-Australia critical minerals cooperation is a policy success: agreed problem → multilateral partnerships → industrial cooperation → price support mechanisms → long-term investment → trade agreements
- The exact same template is available for fuel security — it has just never been applied
- Engineers Australia's 2014-15 recommendations on liquid fuel security followed (almost to the letter) for critical minerals: recast as national capability, not commodity

### The political economy trap
- "Large-scale onshore storage of any commodity is expensive. Industry does not want to foot the bill, and nor should it."
- Government must act — but in a stretched fiscal environment, spending on something "consistently assured to be unnecessary" is politically difficult
- Result: the problem is deferred until the crisis forces action at maximum cost
