## PDF5: AEMO — Gas Statement of Opportunities (GSOO) March 2025
**Publisher:** Australian Energy Market Operator (AEMO)  
**Date:** March 2025 (based on data to 31 December 2024)  
**Scope:** East Coast Gas Market forecasts to 2044  
**Relevance:** HIGH — official government market operator forecast; pre-war baseline for domestic gas supply adequacy  

### Key Claims
1. **Peak day shortfalls forecast from 2028** in southern Australia under high-demand scenarios
2. **Structural annual supply gaps from 2029** — domestic gas production declining; new investment required
3. Southern gas production (Gippsland, Otway basins) declining; reserves downgraded
4. Gas consumption declining overall due to electrification and high retail prices — but electricity generation demand introduces uncertainty
5. **Eraring Power Station** delayed retirement reduces gas-powered generation demand in near term
6. **Carpentaria Gas Field (Beetaloo Basin, NT)** progressed to "anticipated" status — 25 TJ/d from 2027
7. Key storage/transport projects underway: Iona UGS expansion, Kurri Kurri Lateral Pipeline, NGP Reverse Capability (completed Aug 2024)
8. **Queensland LNG producers** advising increased production forecasts to 2028

### Analytical Significance for Assessment
- The AEMO's pre-war forecast already showed domestic gas supply gaps emerging from 2028-2029
- The Iran war has now disrupted global LNG markets (South Pars/Ras Laffan struck), creating a **demand surge** for Australian LNG from Asian customers
- This creates a tension: Australian domestic gas supply is already forecast to tighten; LNG export demand is surging; the government will face pressure to redirect LNG from export contracts to domestic supply
- The Future Gas Strategy (PDF6) commits Australia to honouring long-term contracts — but those contracts are with Japan, South Korea, and China, all of whom are now desperate for supply
- Australia cannot easily redirect export LNG to domestic markets without breaching long-term contracts and damaging its reputation as a reliable supplier

## PDF6: Australian Government — Future Gas Strategy (2024)
**Publisher:** Department of Industry, Science and Resources  
**Minister:** The Hon Madeleine King MP (Minister for Resources)  
**Date:** 2024  
**Relevance:** HIGH — official government policy framework; the strategy against which the war's impact must be measured  

### 6 Guiding Principles
1. Net zero by 2050 — gas as transition fuel
2. Continued gas development needed for energy security and affordability during transition
3. New sources of gas supply needed — declining domestic production
4. Gas use will shift to higher-value/non-substitutable uses (industry, manufacturing)
5. Adapting gas and electricity markets
6. **"Remaining a reliable trading partner for gas and low-emissions gases"** — Australia committed to long-term LNG contracts

### Key Claims
1. "Australia is and will remain a reliable and trusted trade and investment partner, including for LNG"
2. "Australia is committed to meeting our long-term contracts" (explicit government commitment, p.50)
3. Gas needed through to 2050 and beyond under all net zero scenarios
4. Gas is "essential to the transition" — firms renewable power generation, supports manufacturing and minerals processing
5. "Safeguard energy security and affordability" listed as strategy objective
6. LNG export customers "relying on Australian gas to transition their economies to net zero" — Japan, South Korea, China all referenced as partners

### The Policy Contradiction Exposed by the War
The Future Gas Strategy was written assuming stable global LNG markets and orderly LNG export relationships. The Iran war has shattered those assumptions:
- Qatar's Ras Laffan (20% of global LNG) is offline — Australia's LNG customers are now desperate
- South Pars (world's largest gas field) infrastructure destroyed — decade-long supply gap
- The strategy commits Australia to being "reliable" — but domestic supply gaps are forecast from 2028, and the temptation to redirect export LNG to domestic markets will grow
- **The strategy has no war scenario.** Its "energy security" framing is about net zero transition, not conflict-driven supply disruption
- This is exactly the "fair-weather approach" the Lowy "We Told You So" article criticised — the strategy plans for orderly transition, not disruption

### Australian LNG Export Partners
From "How are Australia's LNG exports consumed today?" (p.48):
- Japan (largest customer)
- South Korea

- Other Asian markets
All are now experiencing severe energy stress from the same crisis.
