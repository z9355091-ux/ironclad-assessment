Ironclad Assessment — Edition 1

The Hormuz Paradox

How did Australia become both winner and loser from the same energy crisis — and why does no policy framework exist to manage the contradiction?

Ironclad Intelligence — 21 March 2026 —12 min read

Australia's energy paradox: simultaneously an LNG exporter and a fuel importer with 24 days of diesel.

Executive Summary

Australia dismantled its own energy resilience over 50 years of policy choices — and the Iran war is the bill arriving. The crisis reveals not a geopolitical accident, but a predictable outcome of treating fuel security as a market problem.

Key Judgements

PART 1

The Price Australia Actually Pays

When Australians watch the oil price news, they are watching the wrong benchmark. WTI crude at $97 is a US domestic price insulated by shale production; Australia buys refined fuel priced off Singapore benchmarks (MOPS) which track Oman FOB at $167. This $70/barrel premium is not a temporary arbitrage. It is structural and will persist as long as US shale production insulates American markets from Middle East supply disruption.

The pricing chain is straightforward: Australian refineries are concentrated in two locations (Ampol Lytton in Brisbane with ~130,000 bpd capacity and Viva Energy Geelong with ~120,000 bpd capacity) and produce only 23% of domestic demand, or approximately 250,000 bpd of Australia's 1 million bpd consumption [24]. The remaining 77% is imported as refined product from Singapore (40%), South Korea (20%), and China (15%), with other sources making up 25% [27]. These Asian refineries price off Oman/Dubai crude benchmarks, not WTI. Australia has no optionality in its sourcing and pricing structure. The market structure is locked in by geography, refinery configuration, and distance-driven logistics.

Why the massive divergence between WTI and Oman pricing? The explanation requires moving beyond the "US shale independence" narrative that dominates mainstream commentary. Shale is one factor, not the only factor. The Oman premium reflects a convergence of structural constraints: (1) Asian refineries are configured for medium-sour crude (Dubai API 30-31°, Oman API 33-34°) and require 6-12 months and $100M-500M capital investment to reconfigure for light sweet WTI [1]. (2) Regional export restrictions are tightening the Singapore supply pool, compressing the refined product available for Australian importers [2]. (3) A mine-risk premium is embedded in all physical cargoes transiting Hormuz [22], with shipping insurance costs increasing 4-6 times from pre-war levels [3]. (4) MOPS backwardation (near-term prices higher than forward prices) signals near-term scarcity expectations. Shale insulates the US price; the Oman premium reflects Asian refinery lock-in, regional trade constraints, mine-threat premiums, and scarcity signalling. This is a multi-causal structure, not a shale story alone.

The SPR mirage deserves direct address because it appears in government commentary as a fallback position. Australia negotiated nominal access to the US Strategic Petroleum Reserve in March 2020. This is illusory on three distinct grounds: light sweet crude (WTI specification) is incompatible with Asian refinery hardware (the crude-type mismatch cannot be solved in a crisis timeframe); the SPR is being depleted at 41%, reaching exhaustion within weeks at current drawdown rates [4]; and delivery takes three weeks, putting relief outside the acute shortage window (weeks 1-4 when Australian reserves run empty). When the SPR firewall collapses, WTI converges upward toward Brent and Oman pricing, and the US loses its insulation. Australian planning cannot assume US SPR availability. The 2013 parliamentary assessment that enabled refinery closures was partly based on the assumption that the US SPR would provide backup supply. That assumption has been operationally falsified.

Australia's domestic fuel distribution is now fracturing independently of the global shock. Northern Australia flood events in early 2026 have disrupted fuel supply networks in Queensland and Northern Territory. Independent fuel wholesalers—the backbone of rural distribution—have no term supply guarantees and buy only on spot markets. With oil majors (BP, Mobil, Ampol) withdrawing from wholesale distribution to supply their own retail networks, regional Australia faces acute supply gaps that reflect not global scarcity but broken distribution chains and market concentration. In some Queensland inland towns and Northern Territory communities, fuel has already run out or is rationed. The compound effect of a global supply shock (Hormuz closure, Oman price spike) and a domestic logistics failure (flood-damaged pipelines, major-driven wholesale withdrawal) is qualitatively worse than either shock alone. This is the first time a crisis combines global supply disruption with domestic distribution failure.

The binding constraint is diesel. John Blackburn's analysis — based on government data as of July 2025 — placed on-soil diesel cover at 24 days, a figure the government has neither confirmed nor denied [5]. That absence of official transparency is itself a policy failure. Australia's official reserve position is stated as 30-36 days aggregate, but this masks a product-type vulnerability that the official figures obscure. Diesel—which powers 98% of the transport sector and 83% of coal mining operations [6]—is the fuel facing the worst shortages and highest import concentration. At current consumption rates and accounting for the reserve releases already made by Energy Minister Chris Bowen (approximately 5 days of diesel), actual diesel cover is estimated at 24 days on Australian soil. Rationing becomes unavoidable by week 3-4 if the war extends beyond that window. The official figures of 52-68 days include fuel in transit on foreign-flagged vessels with no enforceable guarantee of delivery in a crisis.

For Australian decision-makers, the correct benchmark is not WTI at $97. It is Oman FOB at $167 and rising [20]. Every government statement referencing the $97 oil price is, intentionally or not, understating Australia's exposure by approximately $70 per barrel. At $167 Oman, Australian pump prices have spiked from pre-war $1.50-1.60/L to current $2.30/L—a 70-cent increase in three weeks. If Oman reaches $200/bbl (plausible by week 3-4 of sustained conflict), Australian pumps approach $2.80-3.00/L and beyond. A filling of a 65-litre tank costs approximately $40 more than three weeks ago. The three-tier price structure created by US shale independence is the first energy crisis in which a major structural factor has created persistent, multi-tier price regimes of this magnitude. Regional price differentials existed in previous crises, but the scale and structural permanence of the current spread is without modern precedent. This difference will persist until either US shale production is disrupted or global LNG supply is restored—neither of which is imminent.

PART 2

The Self-Inflicted Wound

Australia's fuel vulnerability is not a natural disaster produced by geography or geopolitics. It is the compounded result of 50 years of policy choices that consistently treated fuel security as a market problem. In 1973, when the Arab embargo struck, Australia had 6-8 operational refineries meeting roughly 60% of domestic demand, plus domestic crude production from the Bass Strait contributing another 50,000-100,000 bpd [24]. In 2026, Australia has 2 refineries, 23% domestic refining capacity (approximately 250,000 bpd of 1,000,000 bpd consumption), and domestic crude production forecast to decline to negligible levels by approximately 2030. Australia is categorically more vulnerable now despite 50 years of technological development. This is not a cyclical tightening. It is a structural deterioration.

The refinery closure timeline is damning in its simplicity. Five of Australia's seven refineries have closed since 2012: Shell Clyde (Sydney, NSW) — announced 2012, converted to import terminal; Caltex Kurnell (Sydney, NSW) — closed 2014; BP Bulwer Island (Brisbane, QLD) — closed 2015; BP Kwinana (Perth, WA) — announced closure 2020, closed ~2022; and ExxonMobil Altona (Melbourne, VIC) — announced closure 2021, closed ~2022–23. One by one, each deemed "economically unviable" against cheaper Asian mega-refineries. The market was right on commercial grounds. It was wrong on security grounds. That gap between commercial rationality and strategic resilience is the entire problem. Each closure made sense to shareholders. Each closure collectively reduced Australia's ability to manage supply disruption. The incentive structure ensured each closure was rational for the individual company and collectively catastrophic for national resilience. Markets optimised for quarterly returns; no one was responsible for the 50-year outcome. Australia faces consequences for it.

The 2013 parliamentary report on the oil refinery industry concluded: "Changes in domestic refining capacity to date will not impact on Australia meeting its liquid fuel requirements. There are reliable, mature and highly diversified international fuel supply chains [7]." The 2013 parliamentary committee did not cause these closures — the first had already been announced before the report was published. What the report did was provide official policy cover: its finding that supply chains were 'reliable, mature and highly diversified' removed any obligation on government to intervene. Each subsequent closure proceeded against a backdrop of official reassurance that nothing needed to change. A single committee's assumption about global supply reliability became the justification for dismantling domestic capacity. That assumption was not tested against geopolitical risk scenarios or strategic disruption modeling. It was treated as stable fact. That assumption has been proven catastrophically wrong.

Warnings were issued and ignored. Engineers Australia told the 2014-15 Senate inquiry: "The policy equivalent of 'the cheque's in the mail' is worrying. Liquid fuel in transit to Australia through some of the world's geopolitical hotspots is not fuel security, it is wishful thinking [8]." The Lowy Institute, Australia Institute, ASPI, and Maritime Union all forecast this problem through 2014-2022 papers and inquiries. No recent government can claim it was not warned. The warning record is extensive, well-documented, and ignored across multiple governments. The response was consistent inaction.

Australia's compliance with IEA 90-day stockholding standards tells the rest of the story. Australia last met the standard in 2011—14 years of non-compliance. The official 68-day figure includes approximately 21 days of fuel on foreign-flagged vessels in transit, with no enforceable mechanism to ensure delivery in a crisis [9]. The actual on-soil cover is 32 days aggregate and 24 days for diesel specifically. When the imports stop, Australia has less than four weeks before critical fuel rationing becomes unavoidable. The 2013 parliamentary assessment that enabled this trajectory is the decision that produced 2026. That decision was neither inevitable nor technically justified. It was a policy choice.

There is a product-mix problem compounding the volume problem. The two remaining refineries (Ampol Lytton and Viva Energy Geelong) are configured primarily to produce petrol, not diesel or aviation fuel. Diesel and jet fuel—the products most critical to national resilience during supply disruption (transport, mining, aviation)—are the products most dependent on imports [10]. Australia has maximised the wrong output. Refineries cannot be easily reconfigured without months of downtime and capital investment. Australia is locked into a product structure that does not match crisis demand priorities. During the acute shortage phase (weeks 2-4), this becomes a critical vulnerability: the two domestic refineries are producing the least-critical fuel type.

Government subsidy of remaining refineries is implicit admission that the market has failed. The Fuel Security Services Payment has provided approximately $40M to Viva Energy alone to remain viable, with continuation talks extending beyond 2027. But reactive subsidy is not strategy. It is the minimum intervention required to avoid complete refinery collapse. Every dollar of subsidy is a public acknowledgment that the market-based reasoning of 2013 was wrong. If the market-based argument were correct, no subsidy would be needed. The fact of the subsidy proves the argument was incorrect.

New Zealand Foreign Minister Winston Peters recently observed that both Australia and New Zealand "ought to have known that some of their oil refineries should have been kept open" and that both countries were "far too cocky about the world circumstance they're in [11]." New Zealand has zero onshore refineries. Australia has two, both surviving on government support. The refinery closure pathway from 2003-2015 was predicated on the 2013 parliamentary assumption that import supply was reliable. Once refineries closed and were decommissioned, surge capacity could not be rapidly restored. Australia made a 50-year vulnerability bet on a single committee's assessment of global supply stability. The 2026 crisis has called that bet.

The vulnerability Australia faces now is not the result of an unforeseeable event. It is the cumulative result of a string of decisions—each individually defensible on commercial grounds, collectively indefensible on security grounds—that left Australia with less resilience than it had in 1973. That is a policy failure without parallel in Australia's peacetime history. The lesson is not that markets failed. The lesson is that security cannot be treated as a by-product of commercial optimization.

PART 3

Two Crises, One Policy Response

The Iran conflict has produced two simultaneous energy crises with fundamentally different recovery timelines, and policy is treating them as a single crisis with a single endpoint. They are not. The Strait of Hormuz (oil logistics crisis) resolves in 2-3 months of war, followed by 2 months of supply chain normalisation (total 4-5 months). But South Pars/Ras Laffan (infrastructure destruction) creates a decade-long global gas supply deficit. These are not competing crises with a common timeline. These are two separate strategic problems requiring incompatible policy responses.

The Hormuz closure is a supply-restriction problem with a known resolution pathway. Even if war continues, the physical closure is temporary. Israeli-Iranian conflict will eventually stabilize through exhaustion, diplomatic breakthrough, or escalation to new equilibrium, mines will be cleared (estimated 2-3 weeks of intensive mine-clearing operations), and shipping will resume. David Leaney (ANU) estimates 2 months of normalisation following conflict cessation [12]. The oil market will recover within a 4-5 month window if diplomatic resolution or military victory produces a ceasefire. Prices will decline. Supply will normalize. This is temporary acute disruption.

But South Pars and Ras Laffan are categorically different. Israel struck Iran's South Pars gas field; Iran retaliated by striking Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility [13]. Both are physical infrastructure destruction on both sides of a shared reservoir, not mere supply restriction. Ras Laffan cost $70 billion to build and produced 20% of global LNG supply [14]. The rebuild is measured in decades, not months. A liquefaction train takes 4-5 years to build and commission. Multiple trains will be needed to replace destroyed capacity. South Pars/North Dome is the world's largest natural gas field, containing 40% of the combined recoverable reserves of the 25 largest fields globally, but at only 10% depletion [15]. The infrastructure destruction on both the Iran and Qatar sides of the shared reservoir means 90% of the gas will remain unextractable for years. This is not a supply constraint. It is an infrastructure constraint.

Arnaud Bertrand's assessment is stark: infrastructure destruction will take at least a decade to recover from; the world's energy picture is "probably changed forever [16]." This is structural, not cyclical. When oil markets normalize and inflation peaks in months 3-6, the gas market will still be in crisis. By 2028, when the oil shock is ancient history and Australian diesel rationing is forgotten, Australia's domestic gas supply is forecast to tighten, and global LNG demand will be competing desperately for Australian production at premium prices for years. Two separate timelines. Two separate policy requirements. One conflict.

Australia's position in these two crises is almost perfectly inverted. Australia is a structural loser in the oil/fuel crisis (91% import dependency [23], 24-day diesel reserve, regional distribution collapse, pump prices spiking from $1.50 to $2.30/L). But Australia is a structural winner in the gas crisis (LNG exporters Woodside and Santos inherit a decade-long market advantage as Qatar's Ras Laffan is offline; LNG prices have doubled; LNG customers—Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, China—are now desperate for supply and will accept higher prices). The same conflict that threatens fuel rationing is simultaneously delivering a decade-long windfall to Australia's LNG exporters.

This creates a stark distributional contradiction that policy has no framework to address. The beneficiaries of the LNG windfall (Woodside shareholders, Santos shareholders, WA government royalties, federal government tax receipts) are entirely different actors from those absorbing the fuel import cost shock (households paying $2.30/L for petrol, regional transport operators, mining companies paying premium diesel costs, agricultural sector facing fuel-driven input cost spikes). Australia has no mechanism to redirect the LNG windfall to address the fuel vulnerability. The federal government's Future Gas Strategy (published 2024) explicitly commits Australia to honouring long-term LNG export contracts with Japan, South Korea, China, and Taiwan: "Australia is and will remain a reliable and trusted trade and investment partner [17]." But AEMO's 2025 Gas Statement of Opportunities—published before the war—forecasts peak day shortfalls in southern Australia from 2028 and structural annual supply gaps from 2029 [18]. The domestic gas outlook and the export commitment are moving in opposite directions. No current policy framework addresses this tension between export obligations and emerging domestic supply constraints.

The cascade effect extends to food security and input costs. Natural gas is the primary feedstock for ammonia production (Haber-Bosch process), which underpins fertiliser manufacturing globally. European natural gas (TTF) has risen 160% since the war began [19]. Fertiliser prices will spike with a 3-6 month lag, hitting Australian agriculture in the planting season that follows this calendar year. Australian agriculture—already stressed by fuel cost increases (diesel prices spiking 50% in three weeks) and flood disruption to Northern Australia distribution networks—faces a third compounding cost: input costs for the season that follows this crisis. This is not simultaneous multiple crises. This is cascading secondary effects from a single primary shock.

The policy gap is the sharpest observation in this assessment. Australia is a simultaneous winner and loser from the same conflict, with no policy framework to manage the contradiction. The LNG export windfall (Woodside and Santos will see multi-year revenue gains, the federal government will see sustained royalty and tax receipts elevation) should logically be the funding mechanism for the structural fuel security investment that decades of government inaction have deferred. Reserve expansion, domestic production investment, import diversification, crisis stockpile expansion—all require capital. That capital is available through elevated LNG revenues. But the connection between the windfall and the vulnerability has not been made in policy. The Future Gas Strategy was written in a net-zero transition framework, not a conflict scenario framework. The choice to link these policy domains is political, not resource-constrained.

What Is Different This Time

Three structural breaks distinguish 2026 from all previous energy crises in the post-1973 period.

First: Geographically differentiated price impact. In 1973, 1979, and 1990, oil-importing nations experienced roughly symmetrical price shocks. All faced similar domestic prices because crude oil is fungible and global markets cleared at a single marginal price. The 2026 crisis is the first in which a major structural factor—US shale independence at 13.7 million bpd domestic production—has created three distinct, persistent price regimes. WTI at $97 is a US domestic price insulated by continental production. Brent at $113 is the European story, reflecting Atlantic basin exposure. Oman FOB at $167 is the Asian and Australian story, reflecting refinery-locked dependence on Middle East crude [20]. These are not transient arbitrage gaps. They reflect structural supply geography that will persist for years. Previous historical analogies (1973, 1979, 1990) provide guidance on supply dynamics; they do not predict Australia's specific price exposure. Australia experiences 50-70 cents per litre higher pump prices than the US for the same crisis duration. Australia reaches inflation crisis 2-4 weeks before the US does, forcing the RBA into rate hikes potentially before the Fed acts. This breaks the historical pattern where all industrial economies faced synchronised crises with synchronised policy responses. Australia's policy window is compressed by geographic price disadvantage.

Second: Physical infrastructure destroyed, not supply restricted. The 1973 embargo and 1979 Iranian Revolution were supply-restriction decisions—political actors chose to withhold supply through export controls or production shutdowns. Crises had single clear timelines: embargo lifted or revolution resolved, export resumption began, supply restored, prices normalised within 6-9 months. The South Pars/Ras Laffan strikes are categorically different. Physical infrastructure—liquefaction trains, processing plants, pipelines—has been destroyed by military strike. This destruction is irreversible without multi-year rebuild. A ceasefire does not magically restore $70 billion in liquefaction capacity. The gas market recovery timeline is completely independent of the diplomatic timeline. For the first time in the post-1973 crisis history, a major energy crisis has two separate, incompatible recovery pathways: the oil crisis (4-5 months, logistics problem) and the gas crisis (decade-scale, infrastructure rebuild). Policymakers expecting oil markets to normalize by month 6 will be shocked when gas prices remain elevated for 10+ years.

Third: Non-proliferation incentive structure inverted. Iran is attacked precisely because it does NOT have nuclear weapons. This is a catastrophic signal to every non-nuclear state in the region: nuclear weapons are the credible deterrent to air strikes and regime change military action. Every non-nuclear regional state is now reassessing that non-proliferation commitment. Darren Lim identifies an 18-month timeline as plausible for Iran to acquire nuclear weapons if it pursues them; Saudi Arabia's program could be activated in parallel [21]. The security logic that was meant to reward non-proliferation — restraint in exchange for sovereignty — has been inverted. Iran was attacked precisely because it lacked a nuclear deterrent. Every non-nuclear regional state now reassesses that calculus. A proliferation cascade—Saudi Arabia, UAE, Turkey, Egypt accelerating nuclear programs—transforms the regional strategic landscape from a single nuclear power (Israel) to a multi-nuclear Middle East within 3-5 years. This is a second-order strategic effect of the 2026 conflict that will reshape regional deterrence for the next 20 years. Ironclad will examine the non-proliferation implications of the Iran conflict in a separate Assessment.

Implications and Outlook

Three scenario branches structure the forward view, differentiated by conflict duration and regional escalation dynamics.

Scenario A—Negotiated Resolution (Low probability, <15%): Ironclad assigns low probability based on three observable conditions: no surviving Iranian diplomatic interlocutor following the leadership strikes of March 2026 [25]; active mine-laying in the Strait of Hormuz continuing as of publication [26]; and the South Pars/Ras Laffan gas infrastructure destruction, which persists as a structural break regardless of any ceasefire agreement. If resolution occurred: war ends within 2-3 months, Hormuz demining begins (estimated 2-3 weeks of intensive operations), Oman FOB retreats to $140-160/bbl as supply chain fears ease, recovery timeline is 4-5 months total from war start to price normalisation. For Australia: pump prices stabilise at $1.80-2.00/L; rationing avoided; inflation spike contained to 1-1.5%; AUD stable; mining and agriculture resume normal diesel costs. This is the best case, not the expected case.

Scenario B—Prolonged Conflict (Moderate-high probability, 40-50%): War continues 4-6 months. US SPR exhaustion forces reserve releases to halt (estimated 34-41% depletion). WTI converges toward Brent and Oman pricing as the US shale insulation firewall collapses. Oman reaches $180-200+/bbl. Supply chain disruption persists. For Australia: pump prices reach $2.20-2.50/L; rationing enforced by week 12-16 (80L per vehicle per week allocation); mining diesel availability drops to 70-80% of requirement (production curtailment); agriculture faces critical input shortages (fertiliser spikes 3-6 months lag); RBA forced into rate hike cycle (inflation 4.5-5%); AUD depreciates below 0.63 USD as interest rate differentials widen; recession risk elevated across export-dependent sectors; 115,000 Australians in the Middle East region face consular vulnerability if conflict expands; domestic inflation reaches mid-4% levels.

Scenario C—Infrastructure Escalation (Low-moderate probability, 15-25%): Further Israeli strikes on Saudi Aramco facilities (Yanbu, Ras Tanura) or Iranian strikes on UAE terminals. Regional conflict expands beyond Iran-Israel axis. Oman reaches $200-250/bbl. Global SPR exhausted. Fuel becomes a rationed commodity (20-30L per vehicle per week hard allocation). Mining production collapses 40-60% (diesel constraint). Agriculture sector under existential pressure. Inflation spikes to 5-6%+. RBA cash rate sustained at 5%+ through the crisis window. AUD depreciates 15-25% (differential interest rates, capital flight, commodity weakness). Recession becomes depression risk. Unemployment rises to 8-10%+. Social unrest from cost-of-living crisis and rationing. Government forced into significant fiscal intervention (subsidies, price caps, emergency spending).

What should decision-makers watch—specific indicators that will update probability estimates? (1) Mine clearance operations in Hormuz: active demining suggests war resolution pathway; continued minelaying suggests escalation. (2) US-Israel diplomatic coordination signals: joint statements suggest controlled escalation; public disagreement suggests loss of control. (3) Iran succession dynamics: new diplomatic interlocutor emerges vs. leadership void persists. (4) SPR drawdown rate: if >2% per week, exhaustion approaches weeks 4-5; if <1% per week, SPR buffer extends duration. (5) WTI/Brent convergence: approaching parity signals US shale insulation firewall collapsing. (6) MOPS premium trajectory: sustaining above $160 suggests regional supply tightness persisting. (7) AEMO domestic gas adequacy notices: trigger emergency protocols for southern Australian supply contingencies. (8) Regional conflict expansion signals: strikes on Saudi/UAE infrastructure upgrade Scenario C probability.

The assessment-level policy observation: Australia's response to the immediate crisis (rationing frameworks, reserve releases, price monitoring) and its structural response (reserve expansion to 90-day standard, domestic production investment, import diversification) are both overdue. The LNG export windfall—Woodside and Santos are recording multi-year revenue elevation—provides, for the first time, a funding mechanism that does not require new fiscal expenditure or budgetary reallocation. Whether that connection between windfall and vulnerability is made is a political choice, not a resource constraint.

Immediate actions are necessary: activate immediate demand management measures including purchase limits, announce true diesel reserve position, suspend or relax fuel quality standards to maximise import throughput, establish demand prioritisation protocols (emergency services and medical > agriculture > essential mining > transport > retail). These are the minimum measures the evidence indicates are necessary in the immediate term.

Structural responses — reserve expansion to IEA compliance, domestic gas development, refining surge capacity, and regional supply diversification — all require sustained capital and political commitment that has been deferred for decades. The LNG export windfall provides, for the first time, a funding mechanism that does not require new fiscal expenditure. Whether that connection is made between the winners and the losers of this crisis remains a political choice, not a resource constraint.

Methodology

This assessment synthesises research from 27 primary and secondary sources including ABC News Daily podcast transcripts (David Leaney, ANU; David Byrne, Melbourne University), wholesale fuel trader accounts, market analyst assessments (The Kobeissi Letter, Arnaud Bertrand), government sources (AEMO, RBA, Australian Government Future Gas Strategy), and think tank analysis (Lowy Institute, Australia Institute, ASPI). Historical precedent research covered the 1973 embargo, 1979 Iranian Revolution, 1990 Gulf War, and 2020 COVID crash. Key limitations: the 24-day diesel reserve figure is sourced to ABC News December 2025 reporting and lacks independent government verification; Australia's exact import source breakdown is estimated (Singapore ~40%, South Korea ~20%, China ~15%) pending official trade data; Scenario timelines are analytical estimates subject to revision as conflict dynamics evolve. Red team flags are addressed directly in the draft: the Oman premium is attributed to multiple factors (shale independence, refinery configuration lock-in, regional export bans, mine-risk premium), not shale alone. The flood compounding effect is explicitly included. Northern Australia distribution disruption is analysed as independent of global supply shock, creating qualitatively worse compound vulnerability.
